UX/UI Reflection - Nielsen Heuristics

Norman + Nielsen

The second heuristic, Match Between the System and the Real World, aligns closest with Norman’s concerns. The correct choice should be the obvious one. The user should know what to do based on lived experience in the real world; the design should not contradict what feels inherent to the user. The example used in this article is the layout of knobs on a stove, and this reminded me of Norman’s example of the refrigerator. The design should explain how the system runs in the most innate way possible; adding a complicated system design for the sake of aesthetics will only cause confusion (which ties into heuristic number 8: Aesthetic and Minimalist Design.)

Challenging My Own Work

My micro-project struggles with the third and seventh heuristics. The third heuristic is User Control and Freedom. While the system is controlled by whether the user presses the ] or [ key, the user has no real freedom when using the system. They can only click to make the circle grow, or click to “hang up.” Even then, the key pressed function may be interfered with by the randomized drop call function, causing the user to lose all progress. This may leave the user feeling like they do not have the control in the system, and that the program is calling the shots in the end. They may grow confused or frustrated by this. The seventh heuristic is Flexibility and Efficiency of Use. There is very little flexibility in my micro-project. You either keep pressing the button to get to the end, or you don’t. There are no shortcuts; novice and expert users alike must go through the same process. The randomized drop call feature ensures that you cannot navigate the system better based on experience with it. This could be tiring for users who frequently use the system.

Preliminary Sketchbook Interface Audit

The sketchbook violates the first heuristic: Visibility of System Status. There is very little that tells the user about the navigation process, or where you are in the navigation. If you click on a sketch, you’re directed to it. You must press the back arrow of your browser to return. An in-sketch navigation menu that is consistent throughout the sketchbook could be helpful for users to understand at what point of the sketchbook they are in, or where in the process of creating the sketchbook the current sketch lies.

The Hardest Heuristic

I believe that the fifth heuristic, Error Prevention, is the hardest to implement realistically. This is because the designer must identify potential errors, slips, or mistakes that a user may make before the system is live. Corrections could be added after observing users over time, but preemptively designing the interface to avoid errors is challenging for someone already familiar with it. The step takes lots of time and dedication in order to ensure all (or most) errors and mistakes are accounted for. Even with this chunk of the process dedicated to this aspect of the design, there can be many minor errors that slip through the cracks and do not arise until user testing, feedback, and sometimes even after the system is published.
