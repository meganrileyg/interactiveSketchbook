Reading Response 1

The Design of Everyday Things

My micro-project affords the user an abstract phone call. It affords the phone to ring as the user presses certain buttons, and affords a conversation at the end when the call is answered. The signifiers include text that tells the user what button to press and when to try again, and a timer that tells the user how long they must wait before beginning again. If a user were to stumble upon my system, the signifiers would be clear as they are written into the design. However, it is worth noting that the signifiers may only be clear to English-speaking people, and the instructions are only listed in that one language. A non-English speaker may clue in when identifying the "]" button on the screen, but it would take longer.

The red circle is one aspect of feedback, because it grows to tell the user how close they are to an answered call. The vibration effect is feedback as it is the response to the correct button being pressed, so the user is clear that the phone is "ringing." The feedback is immediate once the correct button is pressed, as is the feedback of the dropped call when the system begins again. The overall feedback is the answered call, which is not immediate, but builds over time.

There is intentional confusion in the mapping, because if it is a users first time, they will not know how many times the phone must ring before it is answered. They will soon find out that the circle must grow enough, but it could be interrupted by a dropped call, causing intentional frustration. A user may interpret a dropped call as a failure of a system, even though it is a deliberate design choice to support the feeling of the system.

There are constraints in my system, the first being that the call can be dropped at any time, which frustrates the behavior. Another constraint includes that the call can only occur for ten seconds once the screen is full, and will then revert to the original state. The biggest weakness in my project is probably that the signifiers of what button to press do not tell you anything more about the system, simply that if you want to answer the phone, you must press them. Being that you must speak English to understand them to begin with, it does not help that there is no illustration for what "answering" the phone looks like, and it may be a few tries before the user realizes that the growing circle is the process of answering. On top of this, the dropped call element and the ability to hang up the phone (if the user so chooses to do this) depresses that process even further. There is also the fact that there is no real "telephone" iconography, the addition of which may help to make the system less abstract and more digestible for users.

I found the reading extremely interesting, witty, and insightful. It was information I inherently knew, but hadn't had the dots connected. Of course designers have in mind only the process by which they can understand a design, but it's very important as a designer to think about your work as something widely understandable to everyone. It reminded me of a notion that anyone who has ever worked in customer service knows to be true: never underestimate the stupidity of the general public. Norman didn't put it quite like this, but I still think it applies. The examples of all the ways to mess up the design of a simple door stood out to me, and highlighted the idea that interactive design is about changing human behavior, and that good interactive design will dictate how one behaves without them having to think about it.
